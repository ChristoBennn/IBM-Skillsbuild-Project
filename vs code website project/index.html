Forgotten Firearms

Gyrojet Pistol

The Gyrojet was a radically different type of small arm developed by MB Associates in the early 1960s, created by Robert Mainhardt and Art Biehl. Unlike traditional firearms, it fired self-contained miniature rockets (Microjets) instead of conventional cartridges. These microjets were gyroscopically stabilized by angled exhaust ports and accelerated after leaving the barrel.

H&K G11

The Heckler & Koch G11 was an experimental German assault rifle developed from the late 1960s through the 1980s, designed to fire unique 4.73×33mm caseless ammunition. It featured a rotating chamber and an advanced “hyperburst mode” capable of firing three rounds at extremely high speed before recoil was felt, aiming to dramatically increase hit probability. Despite its futuristic bullpup design, low recoil, and innovative technology, the project was ultimately canceled in the early 1990s due to the high cost, technical issues with the fragile caseless ammo, and political changes after the Cold War, leaving it as one of the most ambitious but unrealized rifle designs in modern history.

The Puckle Gun

The Puckle Gun, invented in 1718 by James Puckle, was an early tripod-mounted, flintlock-operated “machine gun” prototype. It used a revolving cylinder with multiple chambers that could be manually rotated to fire successive shots more quickly than standard muskets of the time. Marketed as a “defense gun” for ships against boarders, it even had promotional versions firing round bullets for Christians and square bullets intended to cause more damage against “infidels.” Despite being innovative and centuries ahead of its time, the Puckle Gun was unreliable, slow to reload, and too complex for mass production in the 18th century, so it saw little actual use and remained more of a curiosity than a practical weapon.

Guycot 40-shot Chain Pistol

The Guycot chain pistol, developed in France in the 1870s by Paulin Gay and Henri Guenot, was a highly unusual revolver that used a 40-round chain magazine instead of a traditional cylinder or box magazine. The ammunition—small proprietary cartridges—was linked together in a flexible chain that fed through the gun’s mechanism, allowing for an unprecedented capacity for a handgun of its era. Despite its innovative design and impressive firepower on paper, the Guycot was hampered by weak ammunition, mechanical complexity, and limited practicality, so it never achieved widespread adoption. Today it’s remembered as a fascinating example of 19th-century firearms experimentation.

Zip 22

The USFA Zip .22, released in 2013 by U.S. Fire Arms Manufacturing Company, was a highly compact, polymer-framed semi-automatic pistol chambered in .22 Long Rifle. It was marketed as an affordable, modular, and futuristic-looking firearm with rails for accessories and even compatibility with Ruger 10/22 magazines. However, the Zip quickly gained a notorious reputation for being unreliable, awkward to operate, and prone to malfunctions, with issues like failure to feed, failure to eject, and safety concerns. Production ended only a couple of years after release, and today the Zip .22 is often remembered as one of the most infamous “failed guns” of modern times.

Liberator Pistol

The Liberator pistol was a simple, single-shot .45 caliber handgun produced by the U.S. during World War II in 1942. Made by the Guide Lamp Division of General Motors, about one million were cheaply manufactured from stamped sheet metal and delivered to resistance movements in occupied Europe and Asia. The idea was that a civilian or partisan could use the Liberator to ambush an enemy soldier, take their weapon, and continue fighting with better arms. Crude, with no rifling and very limited durability, the pistol was never meant for prolonged use but rather as a psychological and practical tool of resistance, embodying the concept of giving oppressed people a chance to fight back.

Szecsei & Fuchs Double Barrel Bolt Action Rifle

The Szecsei & Fuchs double-barrel bolt-action rifle, created in Hungary in the late 20th century, is a rare and extremely unusual hunting rifle that combines the power of a double-barrel design with the reliability of a bolt-action system. Instead of one barrel, it features two side-by-side barrels, both operated by a single, massive bolt mechanism that chambers and extracts two cartridges at once. Designed for dangerous game hunting, it allowed the shooter to have a fast follow-up shot without relying on traditional break-action doubles. While mechanically impressive and capable of handling large calibers, the rifle is extremely heavy, complex, and expensive, making it more of a specialty or collector’s firearm than a practical mainstream hunting weapon.

Nock Volley Gun

The Nock Volley Gun, developed in the late 18th century by British gunsmith James Wilson and produced by Henry Nock, was a distinctive seven-barreled flintlock firearm. All barrels were fired simultaneously from a single trigger pull, creating a devastating close-range volley. It was intended for use by the Royal Navy—particularly to clear enemy decks during boarding actions. However, the gun proved impractical: it was extremely heavy, produced massive recoil, and generated dangerous muzzle flash, which risked injuring the shooter and nearby sailors. As a result, it saw very limited service before being retired, but it remains a famous example of early attempts at multi-barrel firepower.

14-Barrel Flintlock

The 14-barrel volley gun, sometimes called the “double Nock,” was an experimental flintlock firearm from the late 18th century that essentially doubled the design of the Nock valley gun. Instead of a single cluster of seven barrels, it featured two side-by-side clusters, giving the shooter the ability to unleash 14 musket balls at once with a single pull of the trigger. Like its smaller cousin, it was conceived as a naval weapon to clear enemy decks during boarding actions. However, the added weight, tremendous recoil, and massive muzzle flash made it even more unwieldy and hazardous than the original Nock. Never adopted in any meaningful numbers, the 14-barrel volley gun survives mostly as a curiosity of firearms history, remembered for its ambitious attempt at multiplying firepower long before repeating rifles were possible.

Duck's Foot Pistol

The duck’s foot pistol was a multi-barreled flintlock (and later percussion) firearm popular in the 18th and early 19th centuries, named for its barrels that spread out like a duck’s webbed foot. Typically featuring four to six barrels angled outward, it was designed to fire all shots simultaneously, making it especially useful for close-quarters defense against multiple attackers. Naval officers, prison guards, and bank guards sometimes carried them to deter groups of enemies at once. While intimidating, the duck’s foot pistol had major drawbacks—its spread made accuracy poor beyond very short range, and reloading was slow. Today, it remains one of the most iconic oddities of early firearms design.

Dumonthier Bowie Knife Revolver

The Demonthier Bowie Knife Revolver, created in the 1860s by French inventor Frederic Demonthier, was a striking hybrid weapon that combined a six-shot percussion revolver with a large Bowie-style knife blade attached beneath the barrel. It was designed to give its user both a firearm and a fighting knife in one package, reflecting the 19th-century fascination with multi-purpose weapons. However, the bulky grip and awkward balance made it unwieldy as both a gun and a blade, limiting its practicality. Only a handful were ever produced, and it was never adopted for military or civilian use, but today it remains a rare and fascinating example of inventive firearms experimentation.

AF2011

The Arsenal Firearms AF2011-A1, introduced in 2012 by Arsenal Firearms of Italy, is a unique double-barreled semi-automatic pistol based on the classic Colt 1911 design. It features two side-by-side barrels, slides, and magazines, firing either simultaneously or separately, effectively doubling the firepower in a single trigger pull. Chambered in .45 ACP (with some versions in .38 Super), it holds two 8-round magazines joined together, giving the shooter 16 rounds total. While it functions reliably and is fully usable, the pistol is extremely heavy, wide, and more of a novelty or collector’s firearm than a practical sidearm. The AF2011 is often showcased at gun shows and in media as one of the most unusual modern handguns ever put into production.

Belton Repeating Flintlock

The Belton repeating flintlock, invented in the 1770s by Joseph Belton, was an early attempt at a repeating musket that worked much like a Roman candle firework: multiple powder charges and balls were stacked in a single barrel, separated by wads, and designed to ignite one after another from a single trigger pull. In theory, this allowed anywhere from six to sixteen shots to fire in sequence without reloading, but in practice it was unreliable and dangerous—sometimes all charges went off at once, bursting the barrel, or some failed to ignite. Although Belton offered the idea to the Continental Congress, the design was rejected, leaving it as a fascinating but impractical step in the evolution of repeating firearms.

Kolibri

The Kolibri pistol, designed by Austrian watchmaker Franz Pfannl around 1910–1914, is famous for being the smallest commercially produced semi-automatic pistol in the world. Chambered in 2.7mm Kolibri, a centerfire cartridge considered the weakest firearm round ever made, the pistol was tiny—meant more as a novelty or gentleman’s pocket piece than a serious defensive weapon. While mechanically clever, its minuscule cartridge lacked stopping power and had poor accuracy, making it impractical for self-defense. Only a limited number were produced before World War I, and today the Kolibri is prized as a rare collector’s item and an oddity in firearms history.

ZB47

The ZB-47 was a prototype submachine gun developed in Czechoslovakia in 1947 by renowned designer Václav Holek. What set it apart from other SMGs of its era was its unusual magazine system—instead of loading from the side, bottom, or top, the ZB-47 used a horizontal magazine that slid into the front of the pistol grip, giving it a very compact and streamlined profile. It was chambered in the 7.62×25mm Tokarev cartridge, the same round used in many Eastern Bloc weapons, and came equipped with a folding stock to make it even more portable. While innovative and ahead of its time, the design was ultimately not adopted, as Czechoslovakia favored more conventional models like the Sa vz. 23. Today, the ZB-47 survives only as an weird experimental prototype, remembered for its originality in postwar small arms design.
